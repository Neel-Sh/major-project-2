Correction vs. Connection

How does educator feedback shape student identity? This research explores the transformative—and sometimes destructive—power of the words teachers choose.

Core Thesis

The Research Question

How does educator feedback—whether supportive or dismissive—shape how students see themselves as writers and learners?

The Approach

Combining peer-reviewed research from Sherma, Truax, and Zhu with real student experiences to reveal patterns in feedback impact.

Growth Mindset

Process-focused feedback nurtures resilience and confidence in students.

Emotional Wellbeing

Harsh feedback can cause lasting psychological harm to developing writers.

Self-Regulation

Scaffolded comments teach students to become independent learners.

Core Thesis

Feedback isn't just about correction—it's about connection.

When educators provide thoughtful, process-based feedback, they nurture motivation and identity. When comments are harsh, vague, and dismissive, they leave lasting impressions that shape how students see themselves as learners—long after the grade is forgotten.

Sherma, 2025

Psychology of Feedback

"Merely giving feedback may not be helpful if instructors neglect the psychology intrinsically associated with it. Corrective feedback tends to discourage aspiring writers."

Truax, 2018

Growth Mindset Language

"Comments like 'I can tell you put a lot of effort into this' help students develop a growth mindset. Correcting and criticizing undermines motivation."

Zhu et al., 2024

Self-Regulated Learning

"Scaffolding feedback provides step-by-step cues and models to help students learn how to generate correct answers independently."

Synthesis

The Pattern That Emerges

Feedback is power. Every word tells a student 'I see your effort'—or 'I don't.' The difference shapes voices for years to come.
